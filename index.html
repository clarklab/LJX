Les Folies des Hommes

I have a memory that is almost like a dream: I'm lying in the technicolor leaves of a dying willow tree. It's dusk and I’m naked, tugging at the tree bark with my toes, ashamed for something I’ve done or someone I've become. I’m covering my eyes but what I’ve seen remains fixed in my head—dusk, cloud trails dripping lilac on the horizon, a crumpled sheet of rice paper hidden in the sheaths of leaves, torn and serrated from the dictionary it belongs to. Pages fourteen and fifteen from the letter A, where you circled Art and, further down Artifice. You had told me none of this was real. “Not the flowers?” You shook your head and spayed your wrist with a bottle and held it up to my nose. You told me the tree is papier mache and when I asked what that was you tore bits from a box of Ramen Noodle and knocked over a bucket of milky water—the same water you poured over my chest and called it a baptism. You fashioned a new branch and again you rebuked me—a sapling, you called it. You showed me how to change the lights from darkness to day and then you zoomed in on my pouting face and said yes, just like that, baby. Just like that.

Johnny Toogood
